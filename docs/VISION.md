# Nekrosol — Game Design Vision Document

> *"The sun is dying. The Ministry says otherwise. Both facts are going to kill you."*

---

## 1. Premise

Nekrosol is a world that should have ended a generation ago. A stellar event — the details suppressed, reclassified, and buried by the Ministry of Truth — flooded the surface with a constant, creeping solar radiation. The skies burned orange. The old cities cracked and cured to glass. What remained was renamed: Nekrosol. The dead sun's world.

Survivors clustered into pockets of shielded civilisation — underground warrens, repurposed reactor tunnels, shielded market districts — all nominally governed by the Ministry of Truth, a bureaucratic oligarchy that controls the flow of information, credits, and rationed medicine with religious precision. The Ministry doesn't deny the radiation. It simply reframes it: a test of purity, a necessary filter, proof of fitness. Those who survive are loyal citizens. Those who don't were already a drain on the collective.

Beneath the Ministry's story, a different world breathes. Black markets hum with contraband anti-rad compounds. Scavengers pick through glassed ruins for pre-event tech worth more than a year's credits. Pilots run cargo across irradiated corridors that official transit won't touch. Hackers scrape truth from encrypted Ministry feeds. Nobody trusts anybody entirely, everybody trusts somebody a little, and the whole fragile machine keeps running on desperation, black humour, and the stubborn mammalian refusal to lie down and die. This is Nekrosol. It's ugly, it's sharp, and it's home.

---

## 2. Player Fantasy

You are nobody. A fresh arrival in a place that chews up nobodies for sport. You have a skill, a bad reputation (or no reputation, which is almost worse), and just enough credits to not be laughed out of the Dustline Tavern.

The fantasy of Nekrosol is the climb. Every credit earned, every radiation scar that doesn't kill you, every Ministry checkpoint you slip through or bribe your way past — it all builds toward something. You become known. Your name moves through back-channel comms before you do. Faction bosses take your calls. You stop being a scavenger pawning junk and start being the person who decides what junk is worth.

It feels like surviving against odds stacked by people who want you to fail. It feels like building a name in a world that burns names to stay warm. And occasionally — beautifully — it feels like sticking it directly in the Ministry's face and walking away while they're still writing the incident report.

## 3. Core Loop

The loop is tight, intentional, and always hungry:

1. **Spend Energy on Missions** — Each mission costs energy (your stamina, your willpower). Street-level jobs burn 1–2. High-radiation salvage runs burn 4–5. Escort contracts push your limits. You choose how hard to push before you need to rest.

2. **Earn Credits, Loot, and XP** — Missions pay out in Ministry scrip (credits), salvaged gear, consumables, and raw experience. Every run teaches you something.

3. **Upgrade Skills** — Spend XP to deepen your archetype. A thug gets harder to put down. A hacker cracks deeper systems. A chemist synthesises compounds that shouldn't exist. Skills open mission options that were previously locked behind a *"you're not qualified for this"* gate.

4. **Unlock Harder Missions** — Reputation and skill thresholds gate high-value contracts. The Reactor District doesn't let amateurs in. Convoy Escort doesn't hire someone who's never run a patrol. You earn access.

5. **Build Faction Rep** — Each mission and choice nudges faction standing. Work for the Ministry and you get official access but the underground distrusts you. Run black-market jobs and the Fringe Runners remember. Rep is currency in a different economy — one the Ministry can't fully control.

6. **Access Restricted Zones and Story** — Faction standing and skill level together unlock locations, mission chains, and lore fragments that tell the true story of what Nekrosol really is. The loop deepens. The stakes grow. The radiation keeps climbing.

## 4. Stats & What They Mean In-World

**Credits (💰)**
The official currency of the Ministry's economy, administered and audited by Ember Bank. Every credit has a serial number. Every transaction is theoretically logged. In practice, the black market runs on credit too — just credits that have been *laundered* through enough hands that the serial numbers are someone else's problem. Credits are power, survival, and leverage. Run out and the options get ugly fast.

**Energy (⚡ — max 10)**
Not just physical stamina — it's the mental and emotional reserves required to act in a world this hostile. The willpower to take another job when your legs don't want to. The focus to run a solar fringe patrol at noon. Energy recharges with rest and the right compounds, but it's always the limiting factor. Every action has a cost in Nekrosol. Energy makes that cost tangible.

**Health (❤️ — max 100)**
Your physical condition. Combat damage, radiation burns, botched surgeries, bad compounds — they all eat into health. Drop to zero and you're out of the game until someone drags you to a medic or you spend everything you have on a revival kit. Health is slow to restore. The world is not designed for healing.

**Radiation (☢️ — max 100, danger threshold: 80)**
Solar exposure accumulates. Every time you push into the Reactor District, every unshielded patrol, every shortcut through an irradiated corridor — it adds up. Below 50, you're cautious and functional. Between 50 and 80, symptoms start: tremors, cognitive fog, mission performance degradation. Above 80, the body begins breaking down — automatic health drain, skill penalties, and the Ministry's quiet interest in your medical records. At 100, you're a statistic. RAD-X compounds and physicist skills are the only things keeping radiation manageable.

## 5. Skills & Archetypes

The 12 skills of Nekrosol cluster into three archetypes. Nobody is purely one thing — the world demands flexibility — but specialisation is what makes you useful, and useful is what keeps you alive.

### Physical Archetype: Force, Grit, and Salvage
*Thug · Thief · Scavenger · Mechanic*

The Physical archetype covers the people who survive by doing things with their bodies that most people's bodies would prefer not to do. Thugs absorb punishment and dish it out with brutal efficiency — the Ministry uses them as blunt instruments; the underground uses them the same way. Thieves are agile and nimble, specialists in not being where the danger is when the danger arrives. Scavengers have an almost spiritual relationship with ruins — they know what's worth lifting and what'll kill you for touching. Mechanics keep the machines running, the rigs moving, and the life support humming, which in Nekrosol makes them quietly indispensable to everyone and officially ignored by everyone.

### Technical Archetype: Systems, Science, and Chemical Reality
*Hacker · Technician · Chemist · Physicist*

The Technical archetype is where intelligence meets application in ways the Ministry finds both useful and terrifying. Hackers breach Ministry data infrastructure and sell the results to people who need the truth more than they need their freedom. Technicians bridge mechanical and biotech systems — the ones who install the black-market cybernetics and keep them from rejecting. Chemists synthesise compounds from whatever's available: stimulants, toxins, antidotes, things with no official name in the Ministry's pharmacopeia. Physicists are perhaps the most unsettling archetype — they've studied radiation long enough to understand it, manipulate its effects, manage mutations, and occasionally steer its consequences in directions that should not be survivable.

### Social/Criminal Archetype: Connections, Leverage, and Movement
*Grifter · Pilot · Medic · Smuggler*

The Social/Criminal archetype operates in the space between people rather than in the space between bullets. Grifters are the grease in every unofficial transaction — cunning, persuasive, and always running three versions of the same story. Pilots have the courage and reflexes to move through corridors nobody sane would fly, making them essential to anyone who needs cargo or people transported without paperwork. Medics are among the most morally complicated figures in Nekrosol — officially servants of the Ministry's health apparatus, unofficially the people who patch you up after you've done something the Ministry would prefer you hadn't. Smugglers are stealth logistics made flesh: moving contraband, people, and information through systems designed to stop exactly that, and doing it quietly enough that the system never gets around to updating its threat models.

## 6. Locations

### Dustline Tavern
The Dustline sits at the boundary between the shielded district and the first belt of irradiated open ground — which is either terrible planning or the whole point, depending on who you ask. It's a neutral zone by an agreement nobody wrote down and everybody respects because the alternative is having nowhere to meet. Bounty boards scroll with work ranging from desperate to suicidal. The bartender knows things. The regulars know more. The back room has hosted negotiations that would end careers if they were ever transcribed. The radiation outside means the clientele is everyone who couldn't get a better address — which means the Dustline has more operational intelligence per square meter than any Ministry facility. Drinks are bad. Company is unpredictable. It's the only place in Nekrosol where you might hear an honest sentence.

### Ember Bank
Ember Bank doesn't look dangerous. That's the point. Clean surfaces, polite staff, the soft sound of financial transactions being logged in real time. Ember Bank is where the Ministry's credit economy interfaces with the actual economy — the one that includes black-market debt registries, compound loans at interest rates that are technically legal but practically predatory, and a vault system that everyone suspects holds more than just credits. Every major faction has accounts here under names that don't appear anywhere else. The Bank is neutral in the same way a knife is neutral: it doesn't care what it's used for, but someone always ends up bleeding.

### Blackglass Market
Named for the glass-fused ruins it was built over and into, the Blackglass Market is the beating, chaotic, half-irradiated commercial heart of Nekrosol's underground economy. Salvaged pre-event tech sits next to freshly synthesised compounds next to information brokers next to things that don't have a clean name in any language the Ministry sanctions. The stalls shift. The vendors move. The Ministry runs occasional raids that somehow never catch anyone important. Radiation levels are elevated but considered acceptable by anyone who does business here regularly, which tells you something about the clientele. Everything is available at Blackglass. The question is whether you can afford it, who else wants it, and how quickly you can move once you've got it.

### Reactor District
The Reactor District is where the city's power comes from, which means it's where nobody with a choice actually goes. The reactors are old, partially functional, and bathed in radiation levels that the Ministry officially classifies as *"within operational parameters"* for *"essential personnel."* Nobody believes this. The essential personnel rotate in and out quickly, which tells you everything about what *"operational parameters"* actually means. The District is simultaneously the most restricted zone in Nekrosol and the most heavily infiltrated — because the reactors control the power, the power controls the shielded zones, and whoever controls the shielded zones controls who survives. Fringe cults have taken root in the deeper corridors. Pre-event technology of extraordinary value is buried in the hot sections. Getting in requires credentials, connections, or a physicist with a very high tolerance for occupational hazard.

## 7. Factions (Vision)

*These factions do not yet exist in the codebase. They are proposed as the narrative and systemic backbone of the faction reputation system.*

### The Ministry of Truth
The governing force. The official narrative. The reason nothing is true. The Ministry controls the credit supply through Ember Bank, information infrastructure through encrypted comms and media blackouts, and physical movement through checkpoints and radiation clearance permits. They're not cartoonishly evil — they're systemically evil, which is worse. Individual Ministry agents are often just doing their jobs. The institution, however, is built on the premise that the population needs to be managed because the truth would cause panic. They're probably right about the panic part. Working with the Ministry offers access to official zones, clean credits, and surface-level safety. It costs you in reputation with everyone who matters underground. The Ministry is always watching. Build that into everything.

### The Salvage Collective
Born from the first scavenger networks, the Salvage Collective is the closest thing Nekrosol has to a working-class union — and like all working-class unions in dystopias, it's been partially co-opted and is fighting for its soul. Members share intel on safe salvage routes, split the risk on high-rad runs, and collectively bargain with Blackglass Market vendors. They're suspicious of the Ministry and openly hostile to anyone who tips raids. The Collective doesn't care about your politics; they care whether you pull your weight and keep your mouth shut. Their rep reward is access to pre-scouted locations, bulk salvage deals, and the kind of mutual aid that's the only real safety net in Nekrosol.

### The Fringe Runners
Pilots, smugglers, and anyone else who makes their living in the irradiated corridors between shielded zones. The Fringe Runners are the underground's logistics network — moving people, compounds, tech, and information through routes that officially don't exist. They have a code (don't burn a client, don't rat a Runner, don't fly hot without warning your crew) and a deep, performative disdain for the Ministry's transit authority. Their aesthetic is worn survival gear and fast vehicles with mismatched components. Their actual skill is extraordinary. The Fringe Runners are the people who know where the checkpoints move before they move. Gaining their trust is slow; losing it is instant. Their rep unlocks smuggling contracts, black-market cargo manifests, and, eventually, the routes into Reactor District that don't go through Ministry gates.

### The Reactor Cult
Nobody joins the Reactor Cult. You end up there. It starts with extended exposure in the hot zones — chemists synthesising in unshielded labs, physicists mapping mutation patterns from the inside, technicians maintaining systems where the maintenance manuals were redacted. Somewhere in the high-rad corridors, a subset of Nekrosol's technical underground started interpreting radiation not as a threat to be managed but as a *transformation* to be navigated. They're not wrong, exactly — high-rad exposure with the right pharmaceutical support does produce physiological changes that are occasionally useful. The Cult maintains the deepest Reactor District access of any faction, has pre-event technical archives nobody else can reach, and occupies a philosophical space between scientific research group and genuinely frightening religious movement. Their rep path is the most dangerous and the most rewarding. They worship what's killing everyone else. In Nekrosol, that might actually make them the most honest faction of all.

## 8. Tone & Art Direction Notes

Nekrosol's visual language is **industrial decay with functional grime**. Nothing is clean. Everything is used, repaired, repainted, and used again. The UI should feel like a terminal interface on hardware that has been in service since before the stellar event — fonts are crisp because someone *chose* clarity, not because the world is bright.

**Palette direction:**
- **Background:** Near-black with warm-grey undertones (`#0d0d0d` – `#1a1614`). Not pure black — that reads as digital. This reads as *carbon.*
- **Radiation indicators:** Amber to deep red (`#d4600a` → `#8b1a00`). Radiation bleeds across the UI as it climbs. At 80%+, UI chrome gains a faint red pulse.
- **Health:** Desaturated green (`#2e7d32` → `#1b5e20`). Not a health-game green — a survival-monitor green.
- **Credits/Economy:** Muted gold/brass (`#b8860b`). Not wealthy. *Scarce.*
- **Tech/Hacking elements:** Cyan (`#0a7ea4` — the current `tintColorLight`) works well as a technical accent. Lean into this as the **hacker/tech archetype colour** specifically. The current `tintColorDark: #fff` reads too clean; evolve toward `#c8d8e0` (worn white, slightly cool).
- **Energy:** Electric blue-white (`#4fc3f7`). Active, kinetic, depleting.
- **Ministry elements:** Institutional grey-green (`#4a5240`). Officious. Unsettling.

**Typography:** Monospace or near-monospace for data/stats (terminal feel). Variable-width for narrative text. All-caps for faction labels and Ministry communications.

**UI philosophy:** Scanline texture at low opacity. Wear patterns on borders. Status bars that flicker at critical thresholds. The interface should feel like it was *built to survive*, not designed to impress.

## 9. Design Principles

These six principles should be the first question asked about every proposed feature:

1. **Every action has a cost.** Energy, credits, health, radiation, reputation — spending any of these is a decision. Nothing is free. If a feature lets players do something without cost, either add the cost or cut the feature.

2. **The Ministry is always watching.** Government surveillance is ambient world design, not just story flavour. High Ministry rep activities should have a visible footprint. Players should *feel* the information asymmetry.

3. **Radiation is the world's heartbeat.** It's not just a damage mechanic — it's the atmosphere, the clock, and the conscience of the game. It should inform UI elements, mission descriptions, location design, and faction identity. When in doubt, ask: where's the radiation in this?

4. **Reputation is permanent.** Faction standing changes should feel meaningful and hard to reverse. The world should remember. A player who burned the Salvage Collective shouldn't be able to buy their way back in easily.

5. **Scarcity is a feature, not a bug.** Nekrosol is a survival game. Resources should feel scarce. The relief of restoring health or banking credits should land. Don't dilute it with abundance.

6. **Dark humour is a survival mechanism.** The tone is grim, but Nekrosol's characters cope through wit, cynicism, and absurdity. Mission flavour text, NPC dialogue, and item descriptions should carry this. The game is desperate; it doesn't have to be joyless.

## 10. Release Milestones (Vision)

### Alpha — *Prove the Loop*
Core gameplay loop is playable end-to-end. Three missions available (Street Begging, Solar Fringe Patrol, Rust Belt Salvage). All four stats functional and displayed. Basic skill system with at least two archetypes partially implemented. Functional Dustline Tavern and Blackglass Market as location contexts. UI is utilitarian — correct before beautiful. Internal test audience only.

**Goal:** Does the loop feel good? Does spending energy and earning credits create the right tension?

### Beta — *Fill the World*
All four locations accessible and narratively distinct. Faction reputation system live with at least two factions (Ministry of Truth + one underground faction). Six missions with branching difficulty tiers. Proper animations and transition effects — the UI should now feel like Nekrosol, not like a prototype. Item system expanded: SPD-1, MED-1, RAD-X functional with compounding effects. Limited external playtest.

**Goal:** Does the world feel real? Do players care about their faction standing?

### 1.0 — *The Full Picture*
All 12 skills fully implemented with meaningful progression gates. All four factions live with distinct rep tracks and unlockable missions. Full economy balance pass — credit scarcity tuned, Ember Bank debt mechanics functional. Polished UI with radiation visual bleed, animated stat bars, and theme-consistent typography. Lore fragment system delivering story through play. Public launch.

**Goal:** Is this a game you'd recommend to someone? Does it have something to say?
